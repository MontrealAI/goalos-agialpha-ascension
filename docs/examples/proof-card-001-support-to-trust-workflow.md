# Proof Card 001 — The Support-to-Trust Workflow

**Public example status:** illustrative public usage example until replaced by a live Evidence Docket.

## The plain-English story

A buyer has a support question. A normal AI reply may sound polite, but it can miss important details: access issues, refund boundaries, duplicate-charge handling, escalation rules, or private-data limits.

GoalOS turns that messy support situation into a reusable workflow:

1. **GoalOS defines the aim:** answer the buyer clearly, safely, and consistently.
2. **A builder improves the workflow:** support reply v1.0 becomes support reply v1.1.
3. **A reviewer checks the evidence:** did the new workflow actually improve the answer?
4. **AGIALPHA coordinates proof work:** sponsor posting, builder claim, proof submission, review, credential, and reputation.
5. **A Proof Card explains the result:** what improved, what was checked, and what stayed private.

## Why this matters

This is not merely an AI answer. It is a proof-backed improvement loop.

**Before:** one reply is created and forgotten.  
**After:** the workflow improves, the proof is reviewed, the builder earns a credential, reputation updates, and future support work can start from a better version.

## What stays private

- Buyer identity.
- Private email contents.
- Paid product files.
- Internal support policy.
- Private prompts and workflow internals.
- Legal, tax, and security records.

## Why AGIALPHA is useful here

A normal buyer does not need AGIALPHA. They buy or use GoalOS normally.

AGIALPHA becomes useful when multiple parties need shared proof coordination:

- A sponsor funds the mission.
- A builder claims the work.
- A reviewer validates the proof.
- A Proof Card is registered.
- A credential is issued.
- Reputation changes.
- Better missions become accessible.

## Public claim boundary

This example does not claim achieved AGI, achieved superintelligence, investment return, legal approval, tax approval, security approval, mainnet deployment, or live customer result. It is a public example of the intended proof-work loop.
